Section A — Taxation & Incidence (Ch 19)

1. A flat-rate income tax imposes 25% on all income over $25,000 (the first $25,000 is untaxed). For a taxpayer earning $80,000, the tax due is:

Correct: $13,750. Taxable income = $80,000 − $25,000 = $55,000. Tax = 0.25 × $55,000 = $13,750. The $20,000 distractor applies 25% to the full income; the $17,500 distractor incorrectly uses $70,000 as the base.

2. Under the same tax structure (25% on income over $25,000), the average tax rate at an income of $45,000 is closest to:

Correct: 11.11%. Tax due = 0.25 × ($45,000 − $25,000) = $5,000. Average rate = $5,000 / $45,000 × 100 = 11.11%. Note: 7.14% is the rate at $35,000; 14.58% is the rate at $60,000.

3. Consider the full schedule below. The tax is best classified as:

Correct: Progressive. Although the marginal rate is a flat 25%, the average rate rises from 0% to 18.75% as income grows — the hallmark of a progressive tax. A flat marginal rate with an exempt threshold creates progressivity through the average rate.

4. Market demand is \(Q_d = 8 - \tfrac{1}{3}P\) and market supply is \(Q_s = P - 8\). The pre-tax equilibrium price is:

Correct: P = 12. Set \(Q_d = Q_s\): \(8 - \tfrac{1}{3}P = P - 8\). Add 8 to both sides: \(16 - \tfrac{1}{3}P = P\). Add \(\tfrac{1}{3}P\): \(16 = \tfrac{4}{3}P\). Solve: \(P = 12\). Then \(Q = 12 - 8 = 4\).

5. A per-unit excise tax shifts supply to \(Q_s = P - 12\). The diagram below shows both supply curves. The new equilibrium price paid by consumers is:

Correct: P = 15. Set \(8 - \tfrac{1}{3}P = P - 12\). Add 12: \(20 - \tfrac{1}{3}P = P\). Add \(\tfrac{1}{3}P\): \(20 = \tfrac{4}{3}P\). Solve: \(P = 15\). New quantity \(Q = 15 - 12 = 3\).

6. Based on the shift from \(Q_s = P - 8\) to \(Q_s = P - 12\), the per-unit excise tax is:

Correct: 4. The tax shifts the supply intercept from 8 to 12 (a difference of 4 in the P-intercept of the supply equation). Equivalently, for the same quantity, the producer now requires a price $4 higher to supply it — that wedge is the tax.

7. Of the $4 per-unit excise tax, the share borne by consumers (as a fraction of the tax) is:

Correct: 75%. Consumer burden = new price − old price = $15 − $12 = $3. Producer burden = tax − consumer burden = $4 − $3 = $1 (producer receives $15 − $4 = $11, down from $12). Consumer share = $3/$4 = 75%. Consumers bear more because demand is relatively inelastic compared to supply.

8. The excess burden (deadweight loss) of this excise tax — the efficiency loss beyond the revenue collected — equals:

Correct: 2. \(\text{DWL} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times \text{tax} \times \Delta Q = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 4 \times (4 - 3) = \mathbf{2}\). Tax revenue = $4 × 3 = $12. Total welfare loss = $14, of which $12 is transferred to government and $2 is pure deadweight loss (the Harberger triangle).
Section B — National Income Accounting (Ch 21)

9. Given the national income data below, calculate NNP at Factor Cost:

ItemValue ($B)
Net Domestic Product at Factor Cost15,480
Net earned income from abroad80
Indirect taxes1,150
Subsidies190
Correct: $15,560B. NNP at Factor Cost = NDP at Factor Cost + Net factor income from abroad = $15,480 + $80 = $15,560B. This step converts domestic (within borders) to national (by nationals) by adding net factor earnings from abroad.

10. Using the same data, NNP at Market Prices is:

Correct: $16,520B. NNP at Market Prices = NNP at Factor Cost + Indirect Taxes − Subsidies = $15,560 + $1,150 − $190 = $16,520B. Market prices include indirect taxes (like sales tax) but exclude subsidies, which lower market prices below factor cost.

12. Using 2016 as the base year, Real GDP for 2017 is (2016 prices: sugar cane $0.80, yo-yos $2.50, rum $10.00, peanuts $2.00, harmonicas $25.00, peanut butter $4.50; 2017 final quantities: sugar cane 180, yo-yos 700, rum 160, peanuts 225, harmonicas 75, peanut butter 85):

Correct: $6,201.50. Multiply 2017 quantities × 2016 prices: 180×0.80 + 700×2.50 + 160×10 + 225×2 + 75×25 + 85×4.50 = 144 + 1,750 + 1,600 + 450 + 1,875 + 382.50 = $6,201.50. Note: $5,969 is real GDP for 2016 (base 2016); $7,395 uses 2017 as base.

13. Using 2016 as base year, real GDP is $5,969 in 2016 and $6,201.50 in 2017. The real GDP growth rate from 2016 to 2017 is approximately:

Correct: 3.90%. Growth = \(\frac{6{,}201.50 - 5{,}969}{5{,}969} \times 100 = \frac{232.50}{5{,}969} \times 100 \approx \mathbf{3.90\%}\). The 4.08% distractor is the growth rate using 2017 as base year — a different answer because the base year matters.

14. When 2017 is used as the base year instead, Real GDP for 2018 equals (2017 prices: sugar cane $1.00, yo-yos $3.00, rum $12.00, peanuts $2.50, harmonicas $30.00, peanut butter $4.50; 2018 final quantities same as Q11):

Correct: $8,130.00. Multiply 2018 quantities × 2017 prices: 225×1 + 750×3 + 180×12 + 225×2.50 + 85×30 + 85×4.50 = 225 + 2,250 + 2,160 + 562.50 + 2,550 + 382.50 = $8,130. This differs from the 2016-base answer ($6,812.50) because 2017 prices are higher.

15. Using 2016 as base year: real GDP 2017 = $6,201.50; real GDP 2018 = $6,812.50. The growth rate from 2017 to 2018 is approximately:

Correct: 9.85%. Growth = \(\frac{6{,}812.50 - 6{,}201.50}{6{,}201.50} \times 100 = \frac{611}{6{,}201.50} \times 100 \approx \mathbf{9.85\%}\). Using 2017 as base year gives 9.94% — growth estimates depend on the choice of base year, which motivates chain-weighting.
Section C — Applied & Graph Concepts

16. In the optimal-pollution diagram, the socially efficient level of pollution is the point where:

Correct: MSB of abatement = MSC of abatement. The optimal level is NOT zero pollution — further reducing the last unit of pollution costs more (MSC of abatement) than the social benefit gained (MSB of abatement). Efficiency requires MSB = MSC. Zero pollution ignores that abatement itself has rising marginal costs.

17. A Pigouvian tax equal to the marginal external cost corrects a negative externality by:

Correct: supply curve shifts left. The tax raises the firm's private marginal cost by exactly the marginal external cost, making the private supply curve coincide with the MSC curve. The new equilibrium output equals the socially optimal quantity. The demand curve is unaffected.

18. Nominal GDP = $12,000; Real GDP (base year prices) = $10,000. The GDP deflator is:

Correct: 120. \(\text{GDP Deflator} = \dfrac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100 = \dfrac{12{,}000}{10{,}000} \times 100 = \mathbf{120}\). A deflator of 120 means the price level is 20% above the base year. The 83.33 distractor inverts the ratio.

19. Nominal GDP rises from $5,000 to $6,000 while the GDP deflator rises from 100 to 125. Real GDP in the new year is:

Correct: $4,800. Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / Deflator) × 100 = ($6,000 / 125) × 100 = $4,800. Real output fell even though nominal GDP rose — all the nominal increase was due to higher prices. This illustrates why we use the deflator: to strip out inflation.

20. A payroll tax is imposed on employers. Labor supply is perfectly inelastic (vertical supply curve). In the new equilibrium, wages received by workers:

Correct: workers bear the full burden. With a vertical (perfectly inelastic) labor supply, quantity supplied cannot change. The tax shifts labor demand down. To restore equilibrium at the same quantity, the take-home wage must fall by exactly the full tax. Legal incidence (who writes the check) is irrelevant — economic incidence depends on elasticities. Workers are trapped by zero elasticity.
